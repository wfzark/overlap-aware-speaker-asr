# RQ17 — Information-Theoretic Bound on Repetition-Detector Sensitivity

> **Label: `experimental/frontier`** — mathematical derivation of the upper bound on
> the sensitivity of any repetition-based hallucination detector on AISHELL-4's
> *diverse* hallucination, and its empirical verification. Closes #909. Builds on
> RQ12 (`results/frontier/router_failure_modes/`, PR #900) and RQ13
> (`results/frontier/diverse_hallucination_detector/`). This document gives the
> full derivation; the script
> `info_theoretic_bound_analysis.py` implements it; `bound_verification.{csv,json}`
> contains the per-track and summary numbers; `FINDINGS.md` gives the executive
> summary.

## 1. Setup

Let a track be the concatenated separated transcript of an AISHELL-4 window. Each
track is generated by an unknown source and labelled as hallucinated iff
`always_separated_cpwer > 1.0` (the RQ12/RQ13 convention; 37 hallucinated / 40
non-hallucinated tracks, of which 64 carry non-empty text — 37 hallucinated / 27
non-hallucinated — and are the population on which the bound is defined). We
model each track as the output of a stationary ergodic source with entropy rate
H.

A *repetition-based detector* is any detector whose statistic S is a deterministic
function of the track text's compressibility, i.e. of H. Concretely:

- **Compression ratio (CR)** — `len(utf8) / len(zlib)` — is a monotone *decreasing*
  function of H (more compressible ⇒ lower entropy rate ⇒ higher CR under
  zlib's encoding). Whisper's `compression_ratio` flag uses the high-CR
  (repetitive) direction.
- **Token-type ratio (TTR), n-gram repetition counts, run-length statistics,
  and normalised LZ complexity `C_LZ = |LZ78(s)| / |s|`** are all deterministic
  monotone functions of H.
- **LZ78 entropy-rate estimator `H_LZ = |LZ78(s)| · log2 |LZ78(s)| / |s|`** is the
  direct statistic, by Ziv (1978)'s consistency theorem for stationary ergodic
  sources.

The deployed CR guard (RQ12) is therefore a repetition-based detector, and so is
its recalibrated variant (RQ13) and any LZ-complexity statistic. **The bound
derived below applies to all of them.** Language-id entropy is *not* a
repetition-based statistic — it is a function of the script-category
distribution, which is invariant to within-script repetition — so it lies
outside the bound (this is the whole point: it is the right detector for the
diverse hallucination precisely because the bound is too low for the
repetition-based family).

## 2. Data-processing inequality

For any detector statistic S that is a deterministic function of the entropy
rate H, the data-processing inequality (DPI) gives

$$
  I(S; \text{hallucinated?}) \;\le\; I(H; \text{hallucinated?}),
$$

i.e. *no deterministic function of H can carry more information about the
hallucination label than H itself*. Because ROC area and the sensitivity at any
fixed specificity are monotone in mutual information for a binary label
(under the optimal decision rule for each statistic), the ROC of any
repetition-based detector is dominated by the ROC of the H-discriminator:

$$
  \text{ROC}_{S}(\text{specificity}) \;\le\; \text{ROC}_{H}(\text{specificity})
  \qquad \forall \text{specificity} \in [0,1].
$$

This is the *empirical DPI bound*: the sensitivity of the LZ78
entropy-rate discriminator itself, calibrated to ≥ 90% specificity on the
non-empty-track subset, is an upper bound on the sensitivity of any
repetition-based detector at that specificity. By monotonicity every
repetition-based S shares the same ROC as H (it just relabels the threshold
axis), so the empirical LZ-ROC IS the operative ceiling.

## 3. Gaussian-equal-variance closed form

The DPI bound is exact but requires estimating the ROC of H non-parametrically.
For a closed-form approximation, assume the entropy rate H is normally
distributed within each class with a common variance (the Gaussian-equal-
variance model). Let

- μ₁ = E[H | hallucinated],  μ₀ = E[H | non-hallucinated],
- σ² = pooled within-class variance,
- Δ = |μ₁ − μ₀| / σ  (Cohen's d).

The optimal H-discriminator at specificity `1 − α` flags H ≥ τ where τ is the
upper-α quantile of the non-hallucinated distribution:

$$
  \tau \;=\; \mu_0 + \sigma \, z_{1-\alpha},
  \qquad
  z_{1-\alpha} = \Phi^{-1}(1 - \alpha) = \Phi^{-1}(\text{specificity}).
$$

(At 90% specificity, α = 0.10 and z_{0.90} = 1.2816, computed by Acklam's
algorithm + one Halley refinement in `inverse_normal_cdf`; scipy is unavailable
under the numpy+stdlib-only constraint.)

The resulting sensitivity is the upper-tail probability of the hallucinated
distribution above τ:

$$
  \text{sensitivity} \;=\; P(H \ge \tau \mid \text{hallucinated})
                     \;=\; 1 - \Phi\!\left( \frac{\tau - \mu_1}{\sigma} \right)
                     \;=\; \Phi\!\left( \frac{\mu_1 - \mu_0}{\sigma} - z_{1-\alpha} \right).
$$

Equivalently, with Δ = |μ₁ − μ₀|/σ (taking absolute value because the bound is
in the optimal direction regardless of which class has higher H):

$$
  \boxed{\;
    \text{sensitivity} \;\le\; \Phi\!\left( \Delta - z_{1-\alpha} \right)
    \;=\; \Phi\!\left( \frac{|\mu_{\text{halluc}} - \mu_{\text{clean}}|}{\sigma_{\text{pooled}}}
                       - z_{1-\text{specificity}} \right).
  \;}
$$

This is the *theoretical bound* (the Gaussian approximation to the DPI bound).
It is tight when the within-class distributions are Gaussian with equal
variance; otherwise it is an approximation. We report both the Gaussian bound
and the empirical DPI bound (the LZ-ROC) — when they disagree, the
Gaussian-equal-variance model is violated and the empirical value is the
operative ceiling.

## 4. Estimating H from a finite track

We use the Lempel-Ziv 1978 (LZ78) parsing as a consistent entropy-rate
estimator (Ziv 1978; Savari 1997; Cover-Thomas Ch. 13). The LZ78 parsing of a
string s incrementally builds a dictionary: at each step it finds the longest
prefix w of the remaining input that is already in the dictionary, emits the
phrase (w, next char), and adds the phrase to the dictionary. Let |LZ78(s)| be
the phrase count.

- **Normalised complexity:** `C_LZ(s) = |LZ78(s)| / |s|` — phrases per character.
  Repetitive text ⇒ C_LZ → 0; random text ⇒ C_LZ → 1.
- **Entropy-rate estimator:** `H_LZ(s) = |LZ78(s)| · log2 |LZ78(s)| / |s|`
  bits/char. This is the leading-order entropy-rate implied by the LZ78 growth
  rate (the dictionary grows as ~ n·H/log n for a stationary ergodic source of
  entropy rate H, so H_LZ → H as n → ∞). For |LZ78(s)| ≤ 1 the log is zero and
  H_LZ = 0 (a degenerate short-text case, flagged in the JSON).

LZ78 is *universal* in the sense that it achieves the entropy rate without
knowing the source distribution, which is exactly the property we need: we do
not have a generative model of hallucinated vs. clean meeting text.

## 5. Bayes-optimal reference-free detector (outside the bound)

To know *how much* information a non-repetition-based detector could extract
from the text, we approximate the Bayes-optimal reference-free text classifier
by a character-bigram likelihood-ratio test (LRT) with leave-one-out
cross-validation:

- For each track i, fit a halluc-class bigram model on all other hallucinated
  tracks and a clean-class bigram model on all other non-hallucinated tracks.
- Both models use Laplace (add-one) smoothing over a **shared vocabulary** —
  the union of all characters seen in either training set plus boundary
  (`^`, `$`) and `<UNK>` tokens. Using the *same* V for both classes is
  essential: with class-specific vocabularies the class with the larger
  alphabet is penalised by the V in the smoothing denominator, which flips the
  LLR sign and yields sub-random performance (this was a real bug in an
  earlier draft of this script — see the comment on
  `_shared_vocab`/`build_bigram_model`).
- LLR_i = log P(text_i | halluc model) − log P(text_i | clean model).
- Calibrate the LLR threshold to ≥ 90% specificity on the full 77 tracks.

The bigram LRT is *not* a repetition-based statistic — it conditions on the
character identity, not just the compressibility — so it can in principle
exceed the bound. It is the empirical "information ceiling" for any text-based
detector on this hallucination, and the reference against which we compare
language-id entropy (H17c).

## 6. Bootstrap confidence intervals

For every reported sensitivity, bound, and gap we draw 10,000 bootstrap
resamples (seed = 42) of the n tracks with replacement and recompute the
statistic. Conventions (matching RQ13):

- The detector **threshold is fixed at its full-sample value**; resamples
  recompute sensitivity at that fixed threshold. Threshold uncertainty is
  excluded (reported as a limitation).
- For the Gaussian bound, each resample recomputes μ₁, μ₀, σ_pooled and the
  bound on the non-empty-track subset.
- For the entropy-rate gap Δ_H, each resample recomputes the difference of
  class means.
- For the empirical DPI bound (LZ-ROC sensitivity), each resample recomputes
  sensitivity at the full-sample LZ threshold.

CIs are the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the bootstrap distribution.

## 8. Honest limitations of the derivation

1. **Gaussian-equal-variance model is violated on this data.** The empirical
   DPI bound (LZ-ROC sensitivity = 64.9% on the non-empty subset) exceeds the
   Gaussian closed form (43.5%). This means H_LZ is non-Gaussian within at
   least one class — most plausibly the clean class, whose variance (1.28) is
   ~5.6× the hallucinated class's variance (0.23), reflecting the
   near-monoscript Chinese clean tracks (H≈4) plus the empty-text degenerate
   case being excluded. **The Gaussian bound is therefore a lower bound on
   the true DPI ceiling on this data, not the operative ceiling.** The
   empirical LZ-ROC is the operative bound; the Gaussian value is reported as
   the closed-form approximation that motivates the H17a hypothesis test.
2. **n = 64 non-empty tracks (37 halluc / 27 clean).** The bound is estimated
   on a single AISHELL-4 meeting (M_R003S02C01). Bootstrap CIs are wide
   (Gaussian CI [30.5%, 60.1%]; empirical DPI CI [50.0%, 80.0%]); the
   qualitative verdicts (H17b, H17c) are robust but the precise numbers
   should not be over-interpreted.
3. **LZ78 entropy rate on short tracks.** Track lengths range 30–200 chars.
   LZ78's consistency guarantee is asymptotic; on short tracks H_LZ is a
   biased downward estimator of H. This bias is the same direction in both
   classes (so Δ_H is less affected than the absolute H values), but the
   Gaussian model assumes H_LZ is an unbiased estimator, which it is not.
4. **Equal-variance assumption fails.** As noted in (1), σ²_halluc ≈ 0.23 ≪
   σ²_clean ≈ 1.28. The pooled σ is dominated by the clean class. A
   Welch-Satterthwaite variant (unequal variances) would give a different
   bound, but on n = 64 the additional parameter instability is not worth
   the gain, so we report the equal-variance form as the canonical
   closed-form bound and the empirical LZ-ROC as the operative one.
5. **The bigram LRT underfits.** With LOO the halluc model is fit on 36 tracks
   and the clean model on 39; many character bigrams appear once, so Laplace
   smoothing dominates and the LLR is noisy. The bigram LRT's 75.7%
   sensitivity is therefore a *lower* bound on the true Bayes-optimal
   sensitivity; a larger training set (or a stronger LM) would lift it. This
   is why language-id entropy (94.6%) can *exceed* the bigram LRT — the LRT
   is data-starved, not the Bayes-optimal detector being weak in principle.
6. **Concatenated vs. max-aggregation.** RQ13 aggregated per-speaker scores
   by MAX (worst-case speaker). This RQ uses the *concatenated* track text
   so the bound and every detector operate on the same text object
   (apples-to-apples). The two conventions give slightly different per-
   detector numbers (RQ13's CR recalibrated sensitivity was 13.5% under MAX;
   this RQ's CR recalibrated sensitivity is 13.5% under concat — the same
   here, but in general they differ). The RQ13 values are cited in the JSON
   for narrative continuity.
7. **Oracle-TextGrid separation, Whisper-tiny only.** The separated tracks
   are oracle (true silence gaps), not real-separator output; a stronger ASR
   model or a real separator may produce different hallucination types
   (carry-over caveat from RQ12/RQ13).
